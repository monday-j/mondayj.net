# Introduction

I really, really like Red Dead Redemption 2.

It is currently sitting on Metacritic as the 9th best game of all time. It deserves all of this, and more. Rockstar Games have created one of the strongest arguments that games are, in fact, art.

The game stretches close to perfection of its formula. From its visual style to its open-world design, from its main story to its side narratives, this game comes closest to any so far in achieving mastery of its form. 

Video games are still a fledgling art form, and we still struggle to find a balance between functioning game systems and the interactive stories we seek to tell. This game stands to me as a study of the ongoing tug-of-war between systems and stories and this game's attempt to come out the other side. If you want to save yourself some reading, I'll let you know early that it doesn't. Perhaps unintentionally, I think it's in how close this game does get to perfection that new and unforeseen cracks begin to show.

I really, really like Red Dead Redemption 2. But it isn't perfect.

# Part One: The Real World

From the very start of the game, you're very clearly told that this is not like other games. In fact, it almost seems like it isn't trying to be a "game" at all. Instead, it's an *interactive story*, and you play the part of Arthur Morgan, a rough-and-tumble cowboy whose cynical remarks betray a difficult life as an outlaw.

Where most games are very happy to couch your actions in abstractions in the name of gameplay, Red Dead Redemption 2 takes the opposite approach, almost pointedly. Every action you take needs to belong in a believable, real-world context, and you can derive gameplay satisfaction from that level of immersion instead. It's in the small things, such as playing animations for very nearly every in-game action you can take, or using in-game books in place of menus, or even in the way you and others' steps linger in the snowfall. It's all done in the name of realism, and as far as we like to talk about "immersive" games, this is one of the best you can find.

Part of how it achieves this is in the open world design. A lot of open worlds, including very, very good ones, feel like playgrounds for players to experiment and explore with a sense of childlike wonder. This game takes away this approach. You are not the master of this world. You are not special. If you wish to assert yourself here, you must do so by either negotiating or fighting for resources like anyone else. 

The game gives you plenty of avenues to do so as well, with plenty of side-tasks you can do whenever you want to, much like other Rockstar games before it. You need money. You *could* get it by hunting, fishing or gathering herbs, and selling your take to the local township for a liveable wage. You might want to build a fortune by becoming the best poker player in the west. Or, much more efficiently, you can just rob folk. The game makes no attempt to shy away from this either, and in fact encourages it by showing multiple ways to do just that in many of its earlier story missions.

The last, and arguably most important, way of establishing realism is through realistic *characters*. If you take a rag-tag group of individuals and place them into a brave new world, how those characters react to their surroundings and to each others' same reactions can make or break an experience. I'm very happy to report that this is Red Dead Redemption 2's strongest point in its favour. 

You spend quite a lot of this game interacting with your "gang", whether it's small interactions in your camp or full missions full of quips and conversations. Arthur has history with all of the members of this gang, not all of them positive. The most important thing that Red Dead Redemption 2 gets right is that these character relationships are *believable*. It achieves this while being witty and entertaining, which is an incredibly difficult task to pull off. 

As the tension between gang members builds, as the story pushes them towards an inevitable messy climax, you can really feel the internal conflict that plagues Arthur and how it affects him and his choices. You begin to *care* about Arthur; not in the way that we simply root for whoever gets the most screen time, but in an empathetic and almost pitying way. You get angry as he gets angry, you feel sad when he is sad. You feel the small and large injustices placed upon him as he feels them. You care for him in a way that no video game protagonist pulls off in quite the same way. Add to this the journal system, which is probably the best choice Rockstar chose to add to this game, where Arthur documents his personal reflections on the missions that *you as the player* just took him on. 

Arthur isn't just a puppet, a self-insert for the player. He's a living, breathing person, who gets to make his own choices, good and bad. And while he can't rebel against you if you decide to, say, make him slaughter an entire town; I found that I never wanted to *make* this choice. My interpretation of Arthur was so far removed from this video-gamey impulse that it never occurred to me to make this choice in the first place.

The game presents a realistic experience in a way that's incredibly intentional, both in terms of how the game was created but also in how you interact with the world. Every interaction with place or people has been put there for a reason, and none of this game feels like just ticking boxes. You interact with the world because you *want* to. Your choices to engage with the game's content are just as intentional of a choice as the developers that put it there. You develop a connection with this world, the people and their places, and in some ways, the real people that put them there. It's a beautiful harmony between the player and a world that simply *is*, and invites you to experience it on your own terms.
