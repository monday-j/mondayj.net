# Introduction

This is a bit of a strange thing for me to write. I've never done a "video game review" before, but I think this game deserves one. 

Writing this review is the entire reason for starting this blog, or at least the catalyst for me to do it properly. This game, since finishing it, has not left my brain space. There are not many games that persist long after the end credits roll. Some only do so after the 2nd, 3rd or 7th play through. For me, games like Breath of the Wild, Disco Elysium and Fallout: New Vegas have cemented established permanent residency in one of my many brain wrinkles. And now, I add Red Dead Redemption 2 to that list, for better or worse.

Some backstory: I played this game well, well long after its release date of late 2018. I started and finished my first playthrough of Red Dead Redemption 2 in February of 2026. Fortunately, I managed to avoid spoilers for the game for the 7-8 year gap between its release and me getting my hands on it. This was not from any active attempts on my part to avoid them, but instead, seemingly a genuine want from the players of this game *not* to spoil its story. If it were spoiled for me I could not say I would have enjoyed the game as much as I did.

I also had not played the first entry in the series, Red Dead Redemption 1, which is likely to affect my reception of the game vs. those who have played it.

# Visuals and Gamefeel

I'm going to mention this only once throughout the entire review: this game is gorgeous. This is the best-looking game I have ever played and it isn't close. I could write an entire review on this game's visual style alone, which I'm not going to do, just know that it really does look as good as people say it does.

Something else that is often discussed is the game's animations. The animations are, within the context of most video games, *slow*. The Rockstar developers clearly want its players to take their time in this world, and in their commitment to realism, have made the creative decision to make most animations in this game take roughly as long as they would as their real-life counterparts. I find this to be an interesting creative choice, as it was made around the same time that games like Breath of the Wild would decide to omit animations for simple tasks altogether, such as picking up items and gathering herbs.

Realism seems to be a constant theme that drives this game's creative choices. There's a lot here that I haven't discussed; multiple hour-long discussions around how *realistic* this game is already exist, and that isn't what I want to focus on here. The attention to detail and commitment to realism does have some interesting effects on the game once you get further into it, which I'll discuss a bit later.

# World design

Textural fidelity and gorgeous lighting can only do much on its own; ideally, it serves to complement what is *already there*. In this case, I'm talking about the game's world, a figurative representation of the real-world areas of America's central states. Compared to real life, this map is miniscule, but the game does a good job of making it *feel* large.
